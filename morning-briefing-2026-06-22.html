Section 1 — Intelligence Feed

Policy / Regulatory

FCC "Build America" NPRM would cap municipal ROW/permit fees at actual cost and impose a 120-day pole/conduit shot clock — vote June 25

22/22
Policy / Regulatory🔴 Act Today

What happened: Ahead of its June 25 open meeting, the FCC released a draft NPRM, "Build America: Eliminating Barriers to Wireline Deployments," proposing to limit state and local permit fees for wireline deployment to amounts that "reasonably approximate" the government's actual, direct costs, and to require those costs be objectively reasonable, competitively neutral, and nondiscriminatory. It also proposes a 120-day shot clock for action on requests to access government-owned property such as poles and conduit.

Why it matters: ROW fees and access delays are the single largest controllable cost-and-schedule variable in Gigapower's out-of-footprint greenfield builds across AZ, NV, PA, AL, and FL — this rule directly compresses both.

Action: Direct Government Affairs to draft Gigapower comments supporting the fee cap and 120-day shot clock and file before the comment window closes; brief Permitting this week to model schedule upside against current AZ/NV/PA municipal ROW timelines.

FCC pole-cost guidance flags $534M–$4.63B in BEAD pole expense and presses reverse-preemption states to regulate consistently

18/22
Policy / Regulatory🟡 This Week

What happened: In DA 26-579 (June 11), the FCC issued guidance across 2,053 electric-utility service territories estimating BEAD pole-related costs at $534 million to $4.63 billion, and reminded reverse-preemption states of their duty to regulate pole attachments consistently with FCC rules. Many BEAD-affected poles sit outside FCC jurisdiction with fewer cost guardrails.

Why it matters: Pole-owner rate jurisdiction (FCC §224 vs. state) and make-ready cost certainty determine aerial build economics in PA's NE cluster and any AL/FL aerial segments.

Action: Task Engineering to map pole ownership and rate jurisdiction (FCC §224 vs. reverse-preemption state) for Wilkes-Barre/Scranton this week, and flag any territory lacking a published make-ready cost schedule.

BEAD goes technology-neutral and clears final proposals — AL ($460M), AZ, NV, and PA awards now in execution

20/22
Policy / Regulatory🟡 This Week

What happened: NTIA has approved 54 of 56 final proposals and dropped fiber prioritization for a technology-neutral standard, also stripping non-statutory labor and other requirements. Alabama's $460M proposal funds 63 projects covering ~92,000 unserved locations; AZ ($993M), NV ($417M), and PA ($1.16B) allocations are moving to subgrant execution, with construction running 2026–2030. Florida remains pending.

Why it matters: Technology-neutral BEAD strengthens fixed-wireless/LEO competition in rural edges, but the subgrant-funded fiber ISPs in AL/AZ/NV/PA are exactly the tenant pipeline a wholesale open-access platform can host instead of forcing them to self-build.

Action: Wholesale BD to cross-reference Gigapower's AL/AZ/NV/PA build maps against published BEAD subgrant award areas this week and identify 3–5 subgrantee ISPs to approach as anchor or secondary tenants.

Supply Chain

Fiber supply tightens to a "perfect storm" — Corning sold out through 2026 as AI data centers absorb cable capacity

18/22
Supply Chain🟡 This Week

What happened: Corning's CEO says demand relative to supply leaves the company "quite tight," and at least one manufacturer has sold its entire fiber inventory through 2026. AI data centers require up to 5x the connectivity of traditional hyperscale topologies, and Meta's up-to-$6B Corning agreement further commits capacity. Tariff-driven price normalization is expected only once pre-tariff inventories deplete.

Why it matters: Cable allocation, not permitting, becomes the binding constraint on 2026–2027 pass-rate targets if Gigapower competes for the same glass as hyperscalers.

Action: Construction and Procurement to confirm 2027 cable allocation in writing with primary suppliers within 30 days and secure a second qualified source before Q4 ordering for the AZ Maricopa expansion.

Competitive Intelligence

Open-access peer SiFi Networks files Chapter 11 after UK parent pulls funding

18/22
Competitive Intelligence🟡 This Week

What happened: SiFi Networks America — a citywide open-access FTTP developer operating under the FiberCity brand in California and the Midwest — filed Chapter 11 in Delaware on June 5, listing $1–10M in assets against $10–50M in liabilities. The filing followed its UK parent SNA Ltd. entering administration on June 4 and stripping the debtor of funding the same day.

Why it matters: The most prominent independent open-access wholesaler collapsing validates Gigapower's capital structure advantage — an AT&T/BlackRock balance sheet — but also signals municipalities and ISPs will scrutinize open-access partner solvency harder.

Action: Wholesale BD to build a one-page "balance-sheet strength" differentiator citing the JV's AT&T anchor tenancy and BlackRock infrastructure backing, and deploy it in any municipality or ISP conversation where SiFi or another distressed open-access operator was previously courted.

14/22
Competitive Intelligence🟢 Monitor

Market Trends

Private capital now drives 91% of fiber spend as analysts call 2026 consolidation "inevitable"

15/22
Market Trends🟢 Monitor

What happened: More than 91% of fiber deployment spending is now private-sector capex, with ~85M unique U.S. homes passed by end-2025 and a record 11.8M homes passed in 2025. Analysts forecast 2026 as a record deployment year and call consolidation of sub-scale overbuilders inevitable, with financial buyers a rising share of deal volume.

Why it matters: A consolidating, capital-disciplined market favors scaled open-access platforms that let multiple ISPs share one network rather than overbuild — Gigapower's core thesis — and creates distressed-asset acquisition windows.

Action: Strategy to queue a Q3 screen of sub-scale overbuilders inside or adjacent to Gigapower's five-state footprint as potential network-acquisition or tenant-conversion targets.

Section 5 — My Recommendation

File Gigapower comments on the FCC "Build America" wireline ROW NPRM this week — before the June 25 vote — and operationalize the 120-day shot clock into the AZ/NV/PA permitting model immediately. This is the highest-leverage item on the board because it attacks the two variables that actually govern out-of-footprint greenfield economics: municipal permit fees and time-to-access on poles and conduit. A national rule capping fees at actual cost and forcing a 120-day decision window converts Gigapower's biggest schedule risk into a federally-backed deadline, and it does so across all five footprint states at once rather than market-by-market. Government Affairs should submit comments supporting the fee cap and shot clock with Gigapower-specific data on current ROW timelines; in parallel, Permitting should re-baseline AZ/NV/PA build schedules assuming a 120-day ceiling so Construction can pull forward 2027 pass-rate commitments the moment the rule takes effect. Every other item this week — supply allocation, the SiFi collapse, AT&T's NetworkCo — is real, but none changes the unit economics of a passing the way enforceable ROW reform does.
