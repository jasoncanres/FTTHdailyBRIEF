Policy / Regulatory

FCC pole attachment rules took effect May 8 — 90-day large-order surveys, 30-day contractor approval, 120-day make-ready

20/22
Policy / Regulatory🔴 Act Today

What happened: The FCC's Fifth Report and Order pole attachment rules became effective May 8, 2026. For "Large Orders" (the lesser of 3,000 poles or 5% of a utility's poles in a state), utilities now have 90 days to survey, 29 days to issue make-ready estimates, and 120 days to complete communications-space make-ready after payment. Contractor approval is compressed to a 30-day clock. Utilities are already telling FCC staff that contractor backlogs and rising costs threaten these timelines.

Why it matters: Gigapower's NE PA cluster (Wilkes-Barre/Scranton) is aerial-heavy ILEC/electric joint-use territory where make-ready timing is the binding constraint on build schedule.

Action: Direct Permitting + Engineering to file Large-Order pole attachment applications in Luzerne/Lackawanna this week to start the 90/120-day statutory clocks before incumbent BEAD builds saturate make-ready queues.

Section 5 — My Recommendation

File Large-Order pole attachment applications in Luzerne and Lackawanna counties this week. Two clocks are now running against the NE PA cluster simultaneously, and both favor moving first. The FCC's pole attachment rules went effective May 8 with hard statutory timelines — 90 days to survey, 120 days to complete communications-space make-ready — but those clocks only protect attachers who are in the queue, and utilities are already warning the FCC that contractor backlogs will swamp capacity. At the same time, PA's $1.16B BEAD pool is fiber-dominated with Comcast and Verizon taking nearly half, which means subsidized incumbent builds are about to flood the exact same make-ready queues Gigapower needs in Wilkes-Barre and Scranton. Whoever submits completed Large-Order applications first locks the statutory timeline and the contractor capacity behind it; whoever waits inherits the backlog. This is a permitting-velocity land grab, not a market-entry question — direct Permitting and Engineering to file now, and have Government Affairs cite the May 7 wireline-barrier-removal order as preemption backstop if any joint-use utility stalls.
