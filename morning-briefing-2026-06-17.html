Section 1 — Intelligence Feed

Policy / Regulatory

FCC’s “Build America” NPRM proposes a national framework capping municipal ROW fees and permitting timelines — vote June 25

22/22
Policy / Regulatory🔴 Act Today

What happened: The FCC released a draft Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, “Build America: Eliminating Barriers to Wireline Deployments,” ahead of its June 25 open meeting. It proposes a nationwide ceiling on permitting timelines, ROW authorization fees, and use-of-government-property charges for wireline providers, anchored in Section 253 preemption. Public comments have already topped 3,000.

Why it matters: Municipal ROW friction — fees, MLA delays, moratoriums — is the single largest controllable cost and schedule risk in Gigapower’s out-of-footprint greenfield model; federal preemption of that friction is directly accretive to every market in the pipeline.

Action: Direct Government Affairs to file supportive comments before the June 25 meeting, citing specific permitting-delay and fee data from the AZ/NV builds; coordinate the filing through the JV/Parent liaison with AT&T’s regulatory team to amplify, not duplicate.

FCC opens comment on state pole-attachment regimes; BEAD buildouts to touch ~3.95M poles — comments due July 13

21/22
Policy / Regulatory🔴 Act Today

What happened: On June 11 the FCC issued a public notice seeking comment on whether the 23 states (plus D.C.) that self-certify pole-attachment regulation are doing so “effectively,” flagging inconsistencies as BEAD ramps. The agency estimates BEAD will touch 3,954,030 utility poles across 2,053 territories, with pole costs of $534M–$4.63B. Comments due July 13, replies July 27.

Why it matters: Pennsylvania is a certified pole state and Gigapower’s NE PA cluster (Wilkes-Barre/Scranton) depends on predictable make-ready timelines and rates; AZ/NV aerial segments carry the same exposure.

Action: Task Engineering to pull current make-ready cycle times and pole-owner rate disputes in Luzerne/Lackawanna by July 7 so Government Affairs can file a PA-specific comment before the July 13 deadline.

NTIA’s June BEAD restructure drops “fiber-first,” opens awards to LEO and fixed wireless

18/22
Policy / Regulatory🟡 This Week

What happened: New June guidance restructured BEAD: the original “fiber-first” preference is gone, LEO satellite and fixed wireless are now eligible, and labor, employment, and climate reporting requirements were dropped. 54 of 56 states/territories now hold approved final proposals; construction on first projects starts as early as this summer.

Why it matters: Technology neutrality cedes some rural locations to FWA/LEO but frees fiber capital to concentrate on dense, take-rate-rich passings — exactly the wholesale economics Gigapower underwrites.

Action: Have Wholesale BD re-screen AL and FL BEAD-eligible census blocks against multi-ISP demand thresholds within two weeks; deprioritize any block now likely to be claimed by subsidized FWA/LEO.

Supply Chain

Fiber lead times stretch to ~1 year; ribbon cable at 60+ weeks as AI data centers absorb supply — Corning sold out through 2026

21/22
Supply Chain🔴 Act Today

What happened: Cable lead times have risen to roughly a year versus a normal 8–12 weeks, with ribbon fiber at 60-plus weeks. At least one major manufacturer has sold its entire 2026 inventory. Corning signed a multiyear deal worth up to $6B to supply Meta’s AI data centers, including a new Hickory, NC plant — pulling capacity toward hyperscalers.

Why it matters: Construction schedules in AZ/NV/PA are only as firm as the cable on the dock; a year-long lead time turns a permitting win into a stranded build if procurement isn’t locked ahead of the pull.

Action: Direct Construction and Procurement to confirm allocated cable commitments through Q2 2027 with Corning/Prysmian/OFS this week, and pre-position conduit for any AZ/NV segment scheduled before monsoon-season road restrictions.

Section 5 — My Recommendation

File Gigapower’s comment on the FCC’s “Build America” wireline ROW NPRM before the June 25 vote. This is the highest-leverage move available this week because it attacks the one cost-and-schedule variable that governs the entire out-of-footprint greenfield model — municipal ROW fees, permitting timelines, and MLA delays — at the federal level instead of city by city. A favorable Section 253 framework converts every future market entry from a bespoke negotiation into a standardized, faster, cheaper process, compounding across NV, AZ, PA, AL, FL and every metro after them; the pole-attachment proceeding (comments July 13) is the same fight one layer down and should ride the same evidence package. Have Government Affairs pull hard permitting-delay and fee data from the AZ/NV builds, file through the JV/Parent liaison so the comment reinforces rather than duplicates AT&T’s regulatory filing, and get it on the record while the docket is hot at 3,000+ comments. Permitting and supply-chain actions matter, but they optimize within today’s rules — this one changes the rules.
